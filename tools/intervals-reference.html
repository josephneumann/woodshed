The shapes on the neck

Chord-diagram view — low E on the left, high e on the right; nut at top. ● = the lower (root) note, ○ = the other. These compact shapes stay the same anywhere on the neck — but cross the G–B strings and shift the upper note one fret. Click any diagram to hear it.

Identify by ear

  • Consonance first. Is it stable/sweet or tense? Then use process of elimination within that group.
  • Then distance. 6ths sound wider apart than 3rds — because they are.
  • Sing the gap. Run a major scale up to the note for major/perfect intervals, a minor scale for minor 3rds/6ths/7ths.
  • Imaginary tonic. Hear one note as the root of a key and place the other against it (a tritone = degrees 4→7 pulling home).
  • Riff anchors work but take extra mental steps — lean on them at first, then drop them.

Memorize them in a month

  • Week 1 — Steps: Minor 2nd, Major 2nd.
  • Week 2 — Perfects: unison, P4, P5, octave.
  • Week 3 — Imperfect consonants: m3, M3, m6, M6.
  • Week 4 — Dissonants: tritone, m7, M7.

Inversions

  • Flip an interval (top note down an octave) and: Perfect stays perfect, Major↔Minor, Tritone stays tritone. The two always sum to 12 semitones.
Shapes note: interval fingerings stay the same across the neck except when they cross the G–B strings (the only pair tuned a major 3rd apart, not a 4th) — shift one fret. The Ear Trainer's fretboard shows the real voicing for each one.
